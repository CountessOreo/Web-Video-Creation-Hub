▼ AI Integration
Overview

The incorporation of Artificial Intelligence (AI) into information systems signifies a revolutionary shift in how businesses harness technology to elevate decision-making processes, automate tasks, and extract meaningful insights from data. AI integration entails the incorporation of machine learning algorithms, natural language processing, and other AI functionalities into pre-existing information systems, rendering them more intelligent, adaptable, and proficient in executing intricate tasks. This transformative integration has the capacity to reshape multiple facets of business operations, spanning from customer service to data analysis and strategic planning. By infusing AI capabilities, organizations can augment the efficiency and accuracy of decision-making, automate routine processes for increased productivity, and unlock novel possibilities for data-driven innovation. The integration of AI into information systems stands as a pivotal advancement, heralding a new era of enhanced functionality and intelligence across various business domains./p>

Integration of Artificial Intelligence

Artificial intelligence creates machine systems that mimic human intelligence when performing activities such as reasoning, learning, and decision-making. (al, 2023)

AI uses a system of machine learning and deep learning to identify patterns and make deductive reasoning from collated data such as images, text, and audio.

Generative AI is thus an algorithm capable of ‘learning’ or producing new content from the collated data. ChatGPT uses this technology through its language model and has rapidly garnered mass interest from the public. GAI can gather vast amounts of data points to give a predictive response more accurately, which according to Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella has made their company’s standpoint clear that Generative AI is the future of data mining.

The Role of AI Integration

Information systems provide a specific function within the operation of a business, such as transaction processing, information management, decision-making resource management, and executive information management. AI has revolutionized the Information System world, by transforming every industry in which it is applied. (Mitchell, 2023)

AI has heavily impacted Information Technology by automating those actions which are repetitive, or which require little to no human interactions.

AI has most effectively assisted the goal of organizations to achieve a more competitive advantage in business through improved information system activities in the areas of: -

  • Data Analytics
    • It has improved trend forecasting as it can analyse vast amounts of data quickly, thus reducing business decision-making timescales and leading to improved use of profit-making opportunities.
  • Cybersecurity
    • Cyber threats can be effectively dealt with through AI-enhanced systems that constantly monitor an organization’s online data and protect it from ever increasingly nefarious threats.
    • AI can ensure that updates are done to cyber software as soon as new versions or releases are made available. This can be a great selling point for customer ‘peace-of-mind’ which may be a deciding factor in business deals.
  • Chatbots
    • Online customer care has improved using Chatbots which can instantly connect with potential clients and respond to their inquiries or answer any frequently asked questions to encourage the sale of an organization's product or service.
  • Robotic Process Automation
    • Processes that are repetitive or that can be streamlined through automation can be managed by AI software. Tasks can be performed much faster than by a human and with greater accuracy and uniformity through RPA.
    • Tasks that would benefit from RPA, include data extraction, data analysis, data entry, generation of reports, etc.

A staggering 270% increase in deployment by companies of AI technology in their business processes has been recorded over the past 4 years with an estimated 95% integration worldwide expected by 2025. (Biswal, 2023)

The most common applications of AI are:

  • Virtual Assistants such as Alexa or Siri
  • Fraud detection (Chainanalysis) and Cybersecurity (TitanSecure)
  • Self-driving Vehicles (Tesla / SpaceX)
  • Customer Service Interactions (Lyro)
  • Image Recognition (CNN – convolutional neural networks)
  • Healthcare Diagnostics (Merative – IBM)

Future Trends of AI Integration in Information Systems

AI is slated to improve operational optimization by 96%, which will lead to impressive financial benefits. These improvements will take place in areas such as:

  • Automation (83%)
  • Prediction/ Forecasting (70%)

Improvements will be implemented through streamlining processes thus increasing productivity and proactively managing the downtime of machinery. Once consumers are encouraged to accept AI interactions in every business sector of an organization, this trend of improvements will be expedited.

Currently, in South Africa, the fear of job losses due to AI implementation is exceedingly high.

The existing unemployment rate of 32.6% is already perceived as an insurmountable hurdle to active employment opportunities, not to mention adding the possibility (however unfounded) of losing more jobs to AI automation. Consumers also resist automated or self-help systems in customer service, often preferring to speak to a real person rather than follow automated prompts.

As a result, consumers and staff may resist AI since it is an unknown factor, something that always causes people to react negatively at first. To counteract this, education and slow integration methods need to be deployed to stave off mass panic. (L, 2023).

This gradual acceptance can be encouraged through Change management, which is a step-by-step approach to transition an organization's focus, goals, processes, or technology to affect, control, or aid change to improve an organization's profits or reputation. (George Lawton, 2023)

Current and Future Pitfalls of AI Integration in Information Systems

There are several risks and limitations associated with using AI-integrated information systems. These risks can be minimized but need to be noted to ensure that risks are managed.

  • AI Policies

Many countries (including South Africa) do not have adequate or even any regulations or policies in place governing the use of AI in integrated information systems. Companies should ensure that policies are drawn up that encompass any regulations and laws of the country.

This would include aspects such as data protection, industry-specific regulations, and best practice standards relating to AI and machine learning. Should this not occur, the company that uses AI may be liable for claims against it for loss, damage or other negative events directly related to its use.

Rules should be specific regarding data ownership and NDAs for sensitive or restricted information.

These should contain an outline of allowable vendor and third-party interactions to protect against data breaches.

  • AI Research - Authenticity of Sources

With the increased use of AI to gather vast amounts of data from those available on the internet, companies take a risk that erroneous data could be included which could negatively impact business decisions.

  • Data may be from an unreliable source. (Unfounded, disproved, or retracted information is of no value to an organization.)
  • Data may be taken out of context and thus inhibit accurate data modeling.
  • Data may be falsely represented.
    • Example: Steven Pieterse, the CEO of Metisware (a digital education architecture specialist) explains that AI technology has become so advanced that it can impersonate humans which could be used nefariously imitate CEOs or other company decision makers to make fraudulent deals through fake video calls, broadcast app messaging and audio calls.

  • Reliability and accuracy of data

The Dunning Kruger effect is a mental bias whereby some people assume they are experts in a field where their abilities are realistically limited. This causes them to not explore their fields further to stretch the boundaries of the technology within. It is vital that business is provided with reliable and accurate data to better understand their markets and make informed business transactions. Relying solely on AI to gather this information because an organization lacks the intellectual property (limited mental resources) to do so, would be detrimental.

Limitations of AI Integration

  • Availability of Data
    • Limited data can skew data analytics especially when using AI technology. It would be important to establish the origin or source of data before making business decisions. Data may also be inconsistent from different sources or siloed (isolated instances) outliers which may affect the quality of data gathered indiscriminately through AI technology.
  • Resisting AI integration
    • Since AI is a new technology, very few trained individuals have the skills, experience, and training to operate the various AI solutions. This skills shortage means that AI integration is slow or may be incorrectly implemented resulting in resistance or lack of acceptance to the use thereof.
  • Cost Implications
    • When businesses don’t have the available skills and resources may result in companies having to outsource AI data mining. This can become a costly expense which could be easily avoided through upgraded training for existing staff and recruitment / headhunting drives to draw in more qualified staff.
    • Smart Technology can be expensive due to its intricate nature and extensive development investment. This cost can make the implementation and repair or maintenance of technology systems an extreme expenditure for organizations.
  • Implementation, Upgrades and Adaptability
    • All software programs require regular upgrades or revisions. This may cost the company more than the original purchase price of the technology.
    • When upgrading software, or enabling an AI system that is continuously growing, poses the risk that older data (history of transactions, etc.) may no longer be accessible and this needs to be addressed to assure the client of continued service and a maintenance of prior records and transactions. Downtime for systems needs to be minimized as an organization requires up-to-date access to data to make necessary business transactions.
    • AI technologies as with other business systems, may take time to integrate fully, which can result in delays in service delivery.

Ethical Concerns with AI

Experts agree that technologists, ethicists, and policymakers need to address several ethical concerns with regard to artificial intelligence, for example:

  • Algorithmic or Societal Bias and Discrimination in gathered data

    • AI gathers data regardless of intrinsic human bias and thus some included data points may skew analysis. Embedded societal biases may become ingrained in AI algorithms thus perpetuating the bias unintentionally, leading to unfair or discriminating results, for example when assessing new hires, approving loans, meting out criminal justice, or even allocation of business resources.
      • Example: Job applicants may be screened unfairly based on gender or racial bias as AI may use historical data to form a bases of comparison. History may not be as open and tolerant as it is now, which may lead to discrimination based on antiquated rules (i.e., Apartheid was abolished but if AI uses historical hiring methods, the error of including this as a factor, may mean a person could be discriminated against.)
  • Dispelling the mystery of AI through transparency and determining AI Accountability

    • AI may seem a mystery as it is a new technology, the boundaries of which are still being explored. This means that there is still a lot of uncertainty about how AI works and how it derives courses of action or makes decisions. This is known as a “black box” system. For some industries like healthcare, it is vital to understand how decisions on diagnosis are derived or decisions regarding a course of treatment are arrived at. Since AI is being implemented in this field, it is crucial to document how these decisions are being made and who will be held liable or responsible should the decision lead to a problem.
    • As AI technology is still in its infancy, errors can occur and thus determining accountability and responsibility for corrective action needs to be established in advance of implementation.
      • Experts are in the process of developing ‘explainable AI’ which will categorise issues such as accuracy and fairness and attempt to limit or eliminate data bias.

  • Creative Process and Ownership of Created Works

    • There are several questions being raised in the creative industry fields whereby AI is being integrated into the creative process. Questions such as:
      • Who is the creator (man or AI)?
      • Who owns the artwork created by AI alone?
      • Who can sell or purchase (who gets paid) in the event of a fully AI generated creation?
      • AI draws on pattern recognition and if it uses pieces of other artwork in its creation, is this considered plagiarism/copyright infringement?
    • These questions and more will need to be examined and a suitable answer will need to be agreed to before industry wide acceptance will take place.
  • Societal Divides and Fake News

    • In all fields and especially in business and politics there is a lot of fake news which may be used to misinform people on aspects of industry to mislead them and perpetrate fraudulent acts upon them.
    • AI algorithms may also be deployed to manipulate opinions and aggravate existing social divisions through the spread of misinformation.
      • For example: Deepfake technology is deployed to generate realistic but false visual and audio files which give fake news or spread misinformation regarding a current issue with a view to manipulate the financial market or to discredit a competitor’s business.
  • Concerns regarding Privacy, continuous unsolicited Surveillance, and data Security

    • It is vital to know that AI gathers vast amounts of data indiscriminately. This process can have privacy repercussions as corporate information and personal data may be caught up in this data mining exercise. This could lead to issues regarding the rights of individuals to privacy and the facilitation of corporate espionage
      • These issues related to the way the data is collected, analysed, stored, or disseminated.
    • Example: In China facial recognition software is being used to unethically monitor their population often without their knowledge or consent. This may lead to legal repercussions for perceived offenders as identified by an AI algorithm.
  • Job losses

    • AI automation is thought to potentially lead to mass job losses, specifically in jobs where repetitive actions (such as factory lines) or data gathering, and analysis occurs. There are many countries including South Africa where the unemployment rate is extremely high and deploying this technology may exacerbate this problem even more.
    • Implementing AI training initiatives and writing policy guidelines may help existing business to integrate the use of AI more easily in their current work processes and thus dispel fears that may be unfounded but be halting economic growth.

If implemented responsibly, artificial intelligence has great and beneficial potential, but as with all things, this technology needs governance and oversight to ensure that it is used responsibly.
